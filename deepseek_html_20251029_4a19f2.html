What is Postharvest Management?

Postharvest management refers to the stage of crop production immediately following harvest, including cooling, cleaning, sorting, packing, and storage. Proper postharvest handling is crucial for maintaining quality, extending shelf life, and reducing losses between harvest and consumption.


Effective postharvest practices can significantly reduce food waste, maintain nutritional quality, and increase market value of agricultural products. This is especially important in developing countries where postharvest losses can reach up to 40% for fruits and vegetables.


Key factors affecting postharvest quality include temperature, humidity, handling methods, and storage conditions. Understanding these factors is essential for anyone involved in agriculture, from small-scale farmers to large commercial operations.

Key Postharvest Techniques

Temperature Management

Proper cooling immediately after harvest is critical to slow down respiration and ripening processes. Different plants require specific temperature ranges to maintain quality during storage and transport.

Humidity Control

Maintaining optimal humidity levels prevents moisture loss (wilting) and reduces decay. Most fruits and vegetables require high relative humidity (85-95%) to stay fresh.

Modified Atmosphere Packaging

This technique alters the composition of gases surrounding the product to slow down ripening and senescence. It involves reducing oxygen and increasing carbon dioxide levels.

Common Postharvest Challenges

Temperature Fluctuations

Inconsistent temperatures during storage and transport can accelerate spoilage, reduce quality, and shorten shelf life. Maintaining the cold chain is essential for perishable products.

Pest and Disease Infestation

Harvested plants are vulnerable to pathogens and pests that can cause significant losses. Proper sanitation and appropriate treatments are necessary to prevent infestations.

Moisture Loss

Transpiration causes water loss, leading to wilting, shriveling, and reduced market value. Proper packaging and humidity control can minimize these effects.

Ethylene Sensitivity

Many fruits and vegetables are sensitive to ethylene gas, which accelerates ripening and senescence. Separating ethylene-producing and ethylene-sensitive products is crucial.
